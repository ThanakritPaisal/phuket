What the provider scoring system actually does

LOMA scores every Phuket provider across five dimensions to decide who gets surfaced as a Hidden Gem. This is a reading of the engine as built, measured by running it over the real catalog rather than by reading its documentation.

Subject loma-app/src/scoring.ts Catalog 1,243 providers · 1,208 scored Method engine executed over live data Date 17 Jul 2026
Headline

Three of the five dimensions work. Readiness never fires any of its three gates — not once across 1,208 providers. Risk cannot reach the threshold its own specification defines for rejection. And every provider currently routed to human review is selected by a hash of its ID, not by anything a reviewer wrote. The engine is internally consistent and deterministic; it is measuring less than it appears to.

The engine, as built

Five dimensions combine into overall_loma_score, and a provider is a Hidden Gem only if all five clear their thresholds at once. The design intent is stated plainly in the file's own header — popularity earns nothing — and the Visibility Gap dimension does honour it. The gap between intent and execution is elsewhere.

What each dimension reads, and what it contributes.
Dimension Weight Reads Status
Locality 0.30 Thai-script name, branch count, chain-name regex, category Works. Inflated: branch count is hardcoded to 1, so every provider takes the +22 single-location bonus.
Quality 0.25 Star rating, review count Rating plus count only — the one approach both specifications ban by name.
Visibility Gap 0.20 log₁₀ of review count, area penalty Works, and is the most defensible dimension. Absolute rather than relative to peers.
Readiness 0.25 Hours, contact, price, language, image Inert. Every input is hardcoded upstream, pinning the output to 79–95.
Risk −0.30 A hash of the provider ID Subtracted rather than gated, so strong locality can offset it. Its one live signal is fabricated.

Read together the three make the shape plain. All three score 88–100 on Locality and exactly 100 on Quality — a Thai family café, a beachside bowl shop, and a 23,000-review Patong restaurant are indistinguishable on four of the five dimensions. Only Visibility Gap separates them, and it is the one dimension doing the work the engine was built for. The café is auto-verified without review; the bowl shop is sent to a reviewer because of its ID.

Findings

Risk, complaint flags and review trend are derived from a hash of the provider ID

Critical

scoreRisk() adds 42 points when hsh("risk|" + p.id) % 100 < 7, and riskFlags() attaches the user-facing string “Repeated complaint pattern in recent reviews” on the same condition. No review is consulted. It fires for a fixed 7% of providers, stably — the same businesses every run.

b1_0537 rating=4.8 reviews=331 → needs_human_review b1_0643 rating=4.4 reviews=6562 → needs_human_review

Because +42 alone clears the risk >= 45 gate, and nothing else can reach it, this hash is the sole cause of all 92 human-review routings. The same pattern drives review_signal_status, freshness_status and last_checked_at. It is clearly demo scaffolding carried over from the v2 prototype — the concern is that it now renders to users as evidence.

scoring.ts:199, 207, 350–356

Readiness is inert: every input is hardcoded before it reaches the engine

Critical

The adapter supplies constants rather than data — lang: "Thai · English" awards the English bonus to all 1,243 providers; priceText: p.price || "Price varies" and contact: p.phone ? "Phone" : "In person" are never falsy. The floor lands at 79, above every threshold.

Two consequences follow. The specification's hard rule — no contact, no opening hours → not tourist-ready — cannot be expressed, because contact is never missing. And the "Price not clearly stated" and "Safety below threshold" flags are dead code, firing 0 times each.

picks.ts:110–124 · scoring.ts:187–195, 210 · copy.ts:53–63

490 providers reach “verified” with no human involved

Critical

verified: true is hardcoded in the adapter. Its comment states that every provider comes from the community-based-tourism registry — true when the catalog held 255 records, false now that 1,124 of 1,243 arrive from OpenStreetMap and TAT. Directory-sourced providers skip the human queue, so 407 TAT listings and 83 CBT records self-verify.

This inverts the specification's stated first principle: AI must never decide “Verified Local” alone. Meanwhile the database records verification_status: "unverified" for every one of the 1,243. The engine believes everything is verified; the data says nothing is.

picks.ts:112 · scoring.ts:365

106 closed businesses are live in the catalog

Critical

business_status holds 85 CLOSED_PERMANENTLY and 21 CLOSED_TEMPORARILY. The field is stripped from the client bundle during finalisation and read by no code anywhere, though the specification makes “not closed” a hard-required readiness field. Tourists can currently be sent to businesses that no longer exist.

finalize_batch.py:44

Quality is star rating and review count — the one method both specs ban

Warning

Both specifications reject this explicitly: “Do not score only by star rating” and “ห้ามใช้ดาวเฉลี่ยอย่างเดียว”. The required inputs — sentiment, recency, consistency, complaint severity, review authenticity — are absent. The dimension also saturates: a median of 95 against a threshold of 72 means it separates almost nothing.

scoring.ts:162–175

The weighting matches neither specification

Warning

Three documents define three different formulas, and the code follows a fourth. More consequentially, both specs and the code subtract risk rather than gating on it — so a risky provider can be carried by strong locality and good reviews, which the revised spec calls out as the reason to gate.

locality quality gap readiness risk v1 spec 0.25 0.25 0.20 0.20 − 0.30 v2 spec 0.30 0.30 0.25 0.15 gate before scoring scoring.ts 0.30 0.25 0.20 0.25 − 0.30

Hidden-Gem thresholds diverge too: both specs call for 70 / 70 / 60 / 60; the code uses 78 / 72 / 55 / 62. No document accounts for the difference.

scoring.ts:331–339, 222–228

The evidence the engine needs is now in the database

Resolved

4,730 Google review bodies were sitting unread in Postgres and shipped to the client on every boot. They are now analysed: 4,200 per-review labels across 823 providers, each claim citing the review index it came from, with complaint type and severity, star-versus-text disagreement, and a grounded visit note.

That is enough to replace the hash with real complaint evidence and to give Quality the sentiment and recency both specs ask for. Alongside it: halal coverage rose from 11 to 23 confirmed places with 14 more queued for verification, and indoor/outdoor resolution went from 8% to 61% of the catalog.

What to do, in order

  1. Gate on closure

    Restore business_status to the bundle and drop non-operational providers from the catalog. Removes 106 wrong recommendations outright.

    One filter · no model involved

  2. Delete the hash; read the complaints instead

    Remove the hsh("risk|"…) branch and review_signal_status, and derive risk from the stored complaint severity. Human review starts reflecting what reviewers actually wrote.

    Evidence already in Postgres

  3. Un-hardcode the readiness inputs, then gate on them

    Pass real hours, contact, price and language through the adapter and add the specification's hard rule. A quarter of the score's weight starts doing work.

    Adapter change · fields already present

  4. Stop auto-verifying

    Replace verified: true with the real verification_status, and reserve verified for human approval. Rename the AI-only outcome to hidden_gem_candidate.

    Restores the spec's first principle

  5. Give Quality the inputs both specs demand

    Feed sentiment, recency and evidence count from the stored labels, and publish a confidence alongside the score — five Google-selected reviews cannot support more.

    823 providers ready now

  6. Make Visibility Gap relative

    Swap the hardcoded log₁₀ range for an exposure percentile within category and area, as the revised spec requires. category and area are already indexed columns.
