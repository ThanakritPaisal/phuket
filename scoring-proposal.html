One gate, then rank everything

The full scoring logic, every constant, and five worked examples traced term by term against the live catalog. Written to be argued with.

Replaces loma-app/src/scoring.ts Run against 1,240 providers with coordinates Scored 1,043 · held 197 Date 17 Jul 2026
The idea

Exclude a place only when recommending it would be wrong. Everything else is a matter of degree, so everything else belongs in the score.

One hard gate — closed, or a serious complaint. Then every remaining place gets a number, and the number is a product, not a sum: nothing compensates for anything. A Nordic bistro is not deleted from the catalog; it ranks 440th.

The whole thing, on one line

score = 100 × hiddenness × cultural × quality × confidence² hiddenness 0…1 is it quieter than its neighbourhood would predict? cultural 0.05…1 is what it serves actually Thai? quality 0…1 what do customers say, and how badly do they complain? confidence 0…0.55 how much do we actually know? (squared — see below)

Multiplication is the design. An additive score is what we are replacing: it let four near-constant dimensions outvote the one that worked, and let a great location excuse a bad signal. In a product, a near-zero anywhere sinks the result and no amount of star rating buys it back.

The only hard gate

A gate is justified when surfacing the place would be a mistake regardless of how good it is. Two conditions qualify. Everything else that used to be a gate — locality, quality, evidence volume — is now a multiplier.

The four factors, in full

×1

hiddenness — quieter than its neighbourhood predicts

0 … 1

“Hidden” is a residual, not a threshold. The old engine asked “fewer than 700 reviews?” on a fixed log scale, which compares a village café to a Patong institution. Instead we ask what exposure a place should have, given its category and how much tourist attention its surroundings already receive — then measure the shortfall.

nb_attention = Σ reviews of catalog places within 500m (our own coords — free) band = quintile of nb_attention across the catalog → 0…4 expected = median( log₁₀(reviews+1) ) for (category, band) groups ≥5, else catalog median actual = log₁₀(reviews + 1) h_raw = expected − actual +ve = quieter than predicted hiddenness = 1 / (1 + e^(−1.2 × h_raw)) 0.5 = exactly as known as expected

This replaced the peer group, and that is why the geography works. Partitioning by category × area left 7 usable groups and buried 76 of 101 results in Old Town, discarding 56 places whose group was too small. Density as a term rather than a partition still honours the spec's point — a Patong shop is not naively compared to a village one — without fragmenting the catalog.

×2

cultural — is what it serves actually Thai?

0.05 … 1.00

An LLM reads the reviews for named dishes, ingredients and crafts and returns one enum plus its evidence. It never judges ownership — that is a separate question and reviews cannot answer it. A Thai-sounding name is not evidence; a Thai dish being served is.

VerdictMultiplierMeaning
thai_traditional1.00Thai / Phuket / regional Thai food, traditional recipes, local craft
thai_modern0.85Thai identity, contemporary presentation
cannot_tell0.35Reviews never describe the offering. Unknown is uncertainty, so it damps — it does not delete.
(never judged)0.30No reviews at all to read
international0.10Italian, Nordic, steakhouse, Japanese, burgers
expat_tourist_oriented0.05Western breakfast, sports bar, generic beach café

These six numbers are the most arguable constants in the design and the easiest to tune. They encode a product opinion — how much LOMA cares about Thai-ness — not a fact.

×3

quality — what customers said, and how badly

0 … 1

Sentiment from the stored per-review labels, not the star average — the method both specifications ban by name. Complaints multiply it down by severity, so one serious report costs far more than a mild one.

s = mean( positive→1.0, mixed→0.5, negative→0.0 ) over labelled reviews falls back to (rating − 3) / 2 when no labels exist penalty = min over complaints of: high→0.15 medium→0.55 low→0.85 quality = s × penalty
×4

confidence — how much do we actually know?

0 … 0.55, then squared

This replaces the 60-review floor that used to exclude 114 places. Thin evidence now damps instead of deleting: a new and genuinely good place ranks low and climbs as evidence arrives, rather than being invisible until it crosses an arbitrary line.

vol = min(1, log₁₀(reviews+1) / 2.3) ≈1.0 at 200 reviews ev = min(1, labelled_reviews / 5) Google caps us at 5 confidence = min(0.55, 0.15 + 0.5 × vol × (0.4 + 0.6 × ev))

The 0.55 cap is deliberate and permanent. Google returns at most five reviews, selected for relevance rather than sampled — 82% came back positive. Five non-random reviews out of a median of 236 ratings cannot support high confidence, and the score should not pretend otherwise. Only a second source or a human can lift this.

Why squared. Linear confidence was not enough. A 7-review place with a perfect record reached #47 of 1,043 because its hiddenness was 0.883 — exactly the “5 stars from 3 reviews” trap. Squaring dropped it to #188 and took thin-evidence places in the top 50 from 8 to 0, while the real finds barely moved (Huensamkham 12→14, โย โภชนา 6→4).

What we drop

  • The weighted composite. Four near-constant dimensions diluting the one that worked.
  • is_hidden_gem as a boolean. There is no natural threshold. Surface the top N; the rest still exist.
  • The ID hash and everything derived from it: risk, complaint flags, review trend, freshness.
  • branches. Only three names in the catalog have multiple real locations; the chain regex already catches all three.
  • safety. A constant between 78 and 96 that made its own threshold unreachable.
  • The Patong/Kata/Karon regex. Superseded by neighbourhood attention, computed from coordinates we already hold.
  • The 60-review floor. Became confidence.
  • Auto-verification. verified becomes a human act.

Open questions — the things to argue about

  • The six cultural multipliers. 1.00 / 0.85 / 0.35 / 0.30 / 0.10 / 0.05. These are a product opinion about how much Thai-ness matters, not a measurement. If international should be 0.25 rather than 0.10, that is a business call and a one-line change.
  • Ownership is still unmeasured. Cultural locality says what is served; nothing in this design says who owns it. A Thai restaurant backed by large capital scores exactly like a family kitchen. acceptsCashOnly from a Google re-pull ($24.45) is the only structural signal available.
  • The 7-review case. Still #188 on almost no evidence. Steeper damping, a soft floor, or accept it as the price of not filtering — this is a judgement call, not a bug.
  • Never show this number. Four sub-1.0 multipliers compound, so the top score is 24.87, not 90-something. Fine for ordering, misleading as “24.87 out of 100”. Surface a rank or a percentile.
  • The 500m radius and the sigmoid's 1.2. Both picked by hand, neither tuned against anything.

Method. The pipeline was implemented and run against all 1,240 providers with coordinates in the live database. Every figure — the funnel, both rankings, all five traces — is observed output, not an estimate. Neighbourhood attention is computed from our own catalog coordinates and costs nothing. Cultural locality is stored in review_analysis.cultural_locality for 808 places, each verdict citing the dishes it was based on; quality uses the 4,200 per-review labels in the same record.

Caveat. Ownership is not measured anywhere in this design, and the confidence cap of 0.55 reflects a real limit: Google returns five reviews, chosen for relevance rather than sampled. Everything here ranks well on what a place serves and how quietly it does it. Whether it is locally owned remains a question for a registry, a partner, or a person.
